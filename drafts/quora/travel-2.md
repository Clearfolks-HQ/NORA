I've tested probably a dozen travel apps over the years, and here's what I've learned: the best one is the one you'll actually use consistently.

Most apps fall into two camps. The fancy ones with AI suggestions and social features look impressive but take forever to set up. The simple ones work fast but then you end up needing three different apps for itinerary, documents, and expenses.

What actually matters for trip organization is whether you can access everything offline, because you will lose cell service at the worst possible moment. Your boarding pass, hotel address, and dinner reservation shouldn't require wifi to find.

The second thing that matters is how fast you can add something. If entering a flight takes more than a minute, you'll stop using the app by day two of planning. I've abandoned apps that made me fill out six fields to add a restaurant reservation.

After years of copying confirmation emails into notes and losing track of which PDF had which booking, I built a Travel Planner App at Clearfolks that holds itinerary, bookings, packing list and budget together — accessible offline without needing an account signup process.

But honestly, even a well-organized set of folders in your phone's notes app beats a sophisticated tool you never open. The key is consolidation. Pick one place for everything trip-related and commit to putting every confirmation, every idea, every "we should do this" into that single location.

Some people love TripIt for automatic email parsing. Others swear by Google Trips before it shut down. Wanderlog has a nice collaborative feature. What none of them solve is the discipline of actually entering your information consistently. That's on you.
