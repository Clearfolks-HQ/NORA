I've tested probably fifteen meal planning apps over the years, and here's what I learned: most of them are great at organizing recipes but terrible at actually saving you money. They treat the grocery list as an afterthought, something that spits out automatically without any connection to what things cost or what you already have at home.

The apps that save money share a few things in common. They let you see the estimated cost of your weekly plan before you shop. They help you reuse ingredients across multiple meals so you're not buying a bunch of cilantro for one recipe and watching it rot. And they make it easy to adjust on the fly when you realize chicken thighs are on sale but chicken breasts aren't.

What doesn't save money: apps that focus on beautiful recipe photos and complicated dishes with twenty ingredients. Those are cooking apps, not budget tools. Also, any app that requires a paid subscription to access basic features like grocery lists — you'll spend more on the subscription than you save.

I ended up building my own solution at Clearfolks called Meal Planner and Grocery App because I couldn't find something that tied the weekly menu, the shopping list, and the budget together in one place. I wanted to see the whole picture before I walked into the store, not piece it together from three different apps.

Whatever you use, the real test is simple: does it help you spend less than you did before? Track your grocery spending for a month using the app, then compare it to the month before. If the number didn't go down, the app isn't working for you, no matter how nice it looks.
