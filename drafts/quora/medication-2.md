This was the exact problem I faced with my mother-in-law, and I'll tell you what actually worked after a lot of trial and error.

The biggest insight was that reminders alone don't solve the problem. We set up phone alarms, pill organizers, even tried those automatic dispensers. She'd still miss doses. Sometimes she'd silence the alarm and forget. Sometimes she'd take the pill but not close the compartment, so later she couldn't remember if she'd taken it or not. The technology wasn't the issue — the system around it was.

What finally worked was combining a few simple things. We got a weekly pill organizer and filled it every Sunday together, which became a small ritual. We put it in the same spot, next to her coffee maker, because she never skipped her morning coffee. And critically, we started logging when doses were actually taken, not when they were supposed to be taken. That way if anyone checked in, they could see the real picture, not the ideal one.

I built Medication Tracker at Clearfolks partly because I wanted a place to record what actually happened each day, plus keep track of refills and which doctors to call when something seemed off.

If your parent lives alone, the stakes are higher. Consider a daily check-in call timed to when they take their most important medication. Not a reminder call — a connection call where taking the medication is part of the conversation. For my family, the accountability piece mattered more than any alarm.

Start with one or two high-priority medications and build the habit there. Perfect systems fail. Simple habits that attach to things your parent already does are more resilient.
