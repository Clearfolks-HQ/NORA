The expensive curriculum platforms are selling convenience, not education. You can absolutely plan a full year without them — families did it for decades before subscription software existed.

Start with your state's requirements. Most states are surprisingly flexible about what counts. You need to know the minimum: how many days or hours, which subjects are mandatory, whether you need to keep attendance or samples of work. This takes an afternoon of research, not a $300 annual subscription.

Next, gather your actual materials. Libraries, used curriculum sales, free resources from state universities, YouTube channels for specific subjects, living books instead of textbooks. The homeschool community has been sharing resources for years. Most of what you need is either free or cheap if you're willing to hunt a little.

Then map out your year loosely. I'd suggest working backward from any hard deadlines (standardized tests, co-op schedules, family trips) and then spacing your subjects across the remaining weeks. Don't plan every day in September — you'll change your mind by October anyway.

I built our Homeschool Planner App because I kept meeting families who wanted this kind of flexibility without paying monthly fees per child, which adds up fast when you have three or four kids.

The planning itself doesn't need to be complicated. A simple grid showing each kid, each subject, and roughly what you're covering each month is plenty. Update it as you go rather than trying to detail the whole year upfront.

One more thing: build in margin. If you plan exactly 36 weeks of content for a 36-week year, you'll feel perpetually behind. Plan for 32 weeks. Use the extra time for deep dives into things your kids get curious about along the way.
