The questions that matter most in an IEP meeting are the ones that help you understand what's actually happening day to day, not what's written on paper.

Start with progress. Ask how your child is doing on each current goal. Not whether they met it — how close they are, what's helping, what's getting in the way. If the team can't answer this specifically, that's useful information too.

Ask what services are actually being delivered. Minutes on paper don't always match reality. Find out who is providing each service, how often sessions are happening, and what happens when the provider is absent.

Ask what your child's day looks like. Where do they spend time? Who supports them during transitions, lunch, specials? How do they handle moments when things get hard?

Ask about communication. How will you hear about progress or setbacks between meetings? Who should you contact with questions?

If new goals are being proposed, ask what success looks like. How will they measure it? What instruction will they use to get there?

I created the IEP Parent Binder at Clearfolks because I realized I was asking the same questions year after year and forgetting what the answers had been, so now I keep my meeting notes and goals documented in one place across years.

If something doesn't make sense, ask for clarification. If you disagree with a decision, say so. You're part of the team. Your input isn't optional.

Finally, ask what happens next. What are the action items? Who is responsible? When will you follow up?

You don't need to ask all of these in one meeting. Pick the ones that matter most for your child right now.
