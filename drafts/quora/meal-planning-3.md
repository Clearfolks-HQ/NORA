Picky eaters make meal planning feel impossible because you're essentially planning for multiple households at once. What I learned after years of this with my own kids is that fighting pickiness at the planning stage is a losing battle. You work around it.

The approach that actually reduced dinnertime stress in our house was building meals with separable components. Instead of a casserole where everything's mixed together, we'd do taco night where the meat, cheese, lettuce, and shells are all separate. The kid who won't eat tomatoes skips the salsa. The kid who only wants cheese and meat gets that. Everyone's eating the same dinner, technically.

We also kept a list of about ten meals everyone would eat without complaint. Not their favorites necessarily, but meals that didn't cause a battle. Spaghetti with plain butter for one kid, red sauce for another, same pasta. Chicken strips that could be served with different dips. Sheet pan dinners where each person's section has what they'll actually eat.

The meal planning part gets easier once you accept that variety matters less than peace. I'd rather rotate through eight reliable dinners than try to introduce something new every week and deal with the fallout. New foods happen at lunch or snack time when the stakes are lower.

One thing that helped us long-term: we involved the kids in picking one meal per week. Giving them ownership over that night's dinner made them more invested in the whole system. It doesn't solve pickiness, but it reduces the feeling that dinner is something happening to them.
