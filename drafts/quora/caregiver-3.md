This nearly broke my wife's relationship with her siblings. Everyone wanted to help, everyone cared deeply, and everyone had completely different ideas about what needed to happen and who should do it.

The families that coordinate well do a few things differently. They have explicit conversations about capacity, not assumptions. One sibling might have flexible work hours but young kids. Another might have money but no time. A third might live closest but travel constantly for work. These constraints matter and pretending they don't creates resentment.

They divide by type of task, not by time period. "You handle medical appointments, I handle finances, she handles the house" works better than "we'll each take a week." Continuity matters when you're dealing with doctors who need to talk to someone who knows the history.

They create a shared record that everyone can see and update. This was the hardest part for us. My wife would come back from a doctor visit with critical information in her head, and her brother across the country had no way to know what happened unless she remembered to call him. I ended up building Caregiver Command Center at Clearfolks partly to solve this exact problem for our family.

The families that struggle usually have one person doing most of the work while others offer opinions from the sidelines. If you're not doing the tasks, you don't get to second-guess how they're done. That's a rule worth stating out loud early.

Also, someone needs to be the coordinator. Not the boss, not the decision-maker on everything, but the person who knows the current state and can answer "what's happening with Mom this week" at any moment. Rotate this role if you want, but don't leave it vacant.
