I've been through dozens of IEP meetings as a parent, and the ones that went well all had one thing in common: I walked in knowing exactly what I wanted to talk about.

Start by re-reading the current IEP at least a week before the meeting. Not skimming — actually reading. Write down what's working and what isn't. Talk to your child about their experience if they're old enough. What feels hard? What feels better than last year?

Gather recent work samples, report cards, and any communication from teachers. If your child gets outside therapy, ask those providers for brief written input. Schools don't always have visibility into private services, and that context matters.

Make a short list of your priorities. Not twenty things — three to five. What do you most want addressed? A goal that needs adjusting? A service that isn't being delivered? A new concern? Write these down on paper you'll bring with you.

I built the IEP Parent Binder at Clearfolks specifically because I kept losing track of past goals, meeting notes, and provider contacts across school years, and I needed one place to see the full picture before each meeting.

Bring a notebook or device to take notes during the meeting. You're allowed to. You're also allowed to ask for a few days to review any proposed changes before signing. Don't let anyone rush you.

If you can, bring someone with you — a spouse, advocate, or trusted friend. It's easier to listen and process when you're not doing it alone.

The goal isn't to walk in adversarial. It's to walk in informed and focused so you can have a real conversation about what your child needs.
