The honest answer is that most parents don't keep their child's IEP paperwork organized. They try for a while, then things pile up in folders or email threads or that one drawer in the kitchen.

The problem is that IEPs accumulate over years. Goals change. Providers rotate. Services get added or dropped. And when something goes wrong — a goal that isn't being addressed, a service that disappeared — you need to know what was supposed to happen and when.

Start simple. Create one folder, physical or digital, for each school year. Every IEP document, evaluation, progress report, and meeting notice goes in that folder. Label things clearly with dates.

Keep a running document where you note down key contacts — case manager, speech therapist, school psychologist — along with any promises made in meetings. When someone says they'll start pull-out reading support in October, write it down.

After each meeting, take five minutes to summarize what was decided. What goals were changed? What services were adjusted? What did you agree to follow up on?

I built the IEP Parent Binder at Clearfolks because I kept losing track of this stuff myself, and I wanted one place to see goals, meeting notes, services, and contacts documented year over year without digging through old emails.

Review your child's IEP at least once between annual meetings. Check it against what's actually happening. If something doesn't match, you'll catch it earlier.

This isn't about becoming a record-keeping expert. It's about having what you need when you need it — at the next meeting, when switching schools, or when your child transitions out of services entirely.

A little structure now saves a lot of scrambling later.
