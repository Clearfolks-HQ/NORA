The hardest part isn't the teaching — it's keeping track of who needs what, when. I've watched homeschool families struggle with this for years, and the ones who stay sane usually do a few things differently.

First, they stop trying to sync everyone perfectly. Your 7th grader and your 3rd grader don't need to be on the same weekly schedule. Some families do well with subject blocks (everyone does math in the morning), but others find that each kid has their own rhythm. The goal is progress, not coordination.

Second, they build in buffer days. If you plan 180 days of instruction and expect to hit all 180, you'll feel behind by October. Plan for 160 and treat the extra days as catch-up or exploration time.

Third, they keep records somewhere that isn't their head. This sounds obvious, but most families I've talked to started with good intentions and a notebook, then lost track by winter break. You need something you can actually maintain when life gets busy.

I built our Homeschool Planner App after hearing the same complaint from too many parents — most planning tools either charge per child or assume you only have one kid at one level, which doesn't match reality for larger families.

The other thing that helps: don't over-plan September in August. Sketch out your year loosely, then plan in detail only 2-3 weeks ahead. Kids change, books don't work out, life happens. Detailed long-range plans usually become guilt documents.

Finally, talk to your kids about what's working. Even young ones can tell you when something clicks versus when they're enduring it. That feedback saves you from pushing through curriculum that was never going to land.
