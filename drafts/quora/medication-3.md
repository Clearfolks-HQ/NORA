Yes, and I'd argue the subscription model for basic medication tracking doesn't make sense for most families anyway.

I spent twenty years in data analytics, and when I started looking at medication trackers for my own family, I was surprised how many wanted $5 or $10 a month for something that's essentially a specialized list. You're not asking for AI diagnosis or integration with hospital systems. You want to know: what medications, what doses, when to take them, when refills are due, and maybe who prescribed what.

The subscription model works for apps that have ongoing server costs because they're doing complex things in the cloud. But tracking your mom's blood pressure medication and your kid's inhaler doesn't require that infrastructure. The data involved is small. The core functionality is straightforward.

I actually built Medication Tracker at Clearfolks as a one-time purchase specifically because I found the subscription fatigue exhausting — you end up paying forever for something you set up once and maintain occasionally.

If you want free options, there are some decent ones, but read the privacy policies carefully. Some make money by sharing your health data with advertisers or research companies, which you may or may not be comfortable with. Others are ad-supported, which can be annoying but is at least transparent.

My advice: figure out what you actually need first. If it's tracking doses for one person, even a notes app or spreadsheet works fine. If you're managing medications across multiple family members, dealing with refills and doctor information and side effects, then a purpose-built tool makes sense. But you shouldn't have to pay monthly for it.

Look for one-time purchase options or truly free apps with clear privacy practices. They exist.
